[Note 6] A representative figure of human competition is Usain Bolt. It can be said that he exceeds his peers across the greatest number of performance dimensions, and that these dimensions are effectively compressed into a single metric.

The reason is that although Wayne Gretzky, Alexander Karelin, Don Bradman, and Efren Reyes all have extraordinary statistics and performances, this analysis further considers the comparability across different competitive domains. For example, as dominant as Karelin was, his performances are not easily comparable across contexts—his excellence is demonstrated under highly specific rules, such as one-on-one competition. By contrast, Bolt holds first-, second-, and third-place historical records across three events in different combinations, and in the three events he entered at the Olympics and World Championships, he won nearly every time. Compared with Bolt’s record quality, the competitiveness of other candidates does not reach the same level. In this sense, Bolt represents a relatively understated benchmark of competitive ability across domains.

Although Bolt has recorded some losses, these can largely be attributed to the nature of the field itself—such as fluctuations in seasonal form, disqualifications under competition rules, or age-related biological decline—rather than direct competitive inferiority within the same race.

In terms of the combination of statistics and narrative legacy, the GOAT of competitive sport might be Gretzky. However, when focusing on outcomes that can be measured on a common scale, the GOAT is the runner who could glance sideways while sprinting. While this may appear almost simplistic, no dramatic narrative or statistical story outweighs the significance of the elite sprinting domain. He may not evoke the same visible emotional reactions in opponents—such as frustration, broken equipment, or dramatic displays often seen in other sports—but such elements are comparatively less relevant in sprinting.

The weight of elite sprinting lies in both its broad participation base and its compression into a single dimension. Other sports involve many different rules and multidimensional measures of performance, whereas sprinting reduces everything to one variable: time. Running itself is not a highly specialized domain, and the competitive base is enormous. Everything an athlete does ultimately serves that single measurement in seconds—technical aesthetics, such as arm swing or stride form, are not directly counted in results. Small factors—such as reduced adrenaline from minor distractions, or changes in body temperature affecting muscle explosiveness—can alter outcomes by as little as 0.05–0.10 seconds. These findings demonstrate the ability to win people over under strict standards; Gautama could not make it into the front-line ranks.

A judgment can only be considered reliable if it has a substantive epistemological foundation. Unlike philosophical concepts that rely heavily on general semantics—such as “己所不欲勿施於人 (Do not impose on others what you yourself do not desire)” and “‎⁦Ἡ ἀγάπη μακροθυμεῖ, χρηστεύεται⁩”—the teachings of Gautama are based on the “Supreme Enlightenment” claimed to have occurred between 450 and 445 BCE, and the entire body of thought encompassing dependent origination, the Noble Eightfold Path, and Nirvana relies heavily on authoritative semantic foundations. As these are symbols unverified by public standards, when there is a discrepancy between the evaluative basis or public standards and the prerequisites for the validity of concepts, it is difficult to say that the public’s evaluation of the Pali Canon is reliable; rather, it amounts more to a veneration of symbolic signs.

As for the order-maintaining function of Buddhism, the ruling class utilized religious thought to maintain order, but the true driving force lay within the ruling class itself; the Pali Canon served merely as a replaceable medium.
